\begin{abstract}
Heart rate variability (HRV) is widely used to assess autonomic
regulation, but standard metrics become difficult to interpret when
assumptions about respiration, signal quality, or window duration are
violated. We examined HRV behavior across postural baseline, voluntary
breath-hold, walking, and paced breathing at five rates between 3 and
12 breaths/min, using fourteen single-lead ECG recordings from one
participant.

Postural loading produced an orthostatic response: heart rate rose from
54 to 80 bpm and RMSSD fell from 99 to 25 ms from supine to standing.
Inspiratory breath-hold produced a larger heart-rate increase than
expiratory hold ($\Delta$HR = +14.3 vs.\ +1.9 bpm). Walking raised heart
rate with rapid recovery, but cadence--cardiac spectral overlap limited
fine-scale HRV interpretation. During paced breathing, the RR spectral
peak tracked the imposed respiratory rate ($R^{2}=0.998$). As breathing
slowed below the conventional 0.15-Hz HF boundary, LF/HF rose from 0.22
to 17.47 while RMSSD remained between 48 and 65 ms, suggesting that
LF/HF primarily reflected spectral-band migration rather than a
straightforward sympathovagal shift. RR oscillation amplitude increased
monotonically with slower breathing, without a clear maximum at 6 breaths/min.

The 60-fold within-participant LF/HF range indicates that LF/HF can be
strongly shaped by task physiology and respiratory frequency. In this
setting, LF/HF should not be interpreted as autonomic balance without
accounting for the respiratory spectral peak.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Heart rate variability quantifies beat-to-beat fluctuations in cardiac
cycle length, driven primarily by autonomic neural modulation of the
sinoatrial node~\cite{taskforce1996}.
Time-domain indices such as SDNN and RMSSD capture overall and
short-term variability, while frequency-domain decomposition into
low-frequency (LF: 0.04--0.15~Hz) and high-frequency (HF:
0.15--0.40~Hz) bands provides a spectral view of autonomic
oscillations~\cite{taskforce1996}.
The HF band primarily reflects respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA),
the phasic vagal modulation of heart rate coupled to the breathing
cycle~\cite{hirsch1981}.

The physiological interpretation of a
given metric depends on the recording condition.
The LF/HF ratio, originally proposed as a sympathovagal balance
index~\cite{taskforce1996}, has been challenged on mechanistic
grounds: LF power reflects both sympathetic and parasympathetic
modulation, and the ratio becomes particularly unreliable when
respiratory frequency falls below the conventional 0.15~Hz boundary
separating LF from HF~\cite{billman2013,eckberg1997}.
Under such conditions, the dominant respiratory oscillation migrates
into the LF band, inflating LF/HF without an actual shift in
autonomic balance.
This limitation is clinically relevant because slow-breathing
interventions are increasingly used in biofeedback
therapy~\cite{lehrer2014}.

Here, we examine how common HRV metrics behave across four task
contexts (steady-state posture, transient breath-hold, ambulatory
motion, and paced respiration) using 14~single-lead ECG recordings
(500~Hz) from one participant processed through a unified analysis
pipeline. The within-subject design eliminates inter-individual
variability, enabling direct comparison of metric behavior across
physiological demands.

We used posture, breath-hold, walking, and paced breathing to address
two related questions: whether standard HRV indices behave as expected
under common physiological manipulations, and whether LF/HF becomes
misleading when respiration crosses conventional spectral-band
boundaries.

Specifically, we expected orthostatic loading to produce monotonic
vagal withdrawal (H1)~\cite{taskforce1996,shaffer2017}; inspiratory
breath-hold to impose a greater chronotropic challenge than expiratory
hold (H2), given higher intrathoracic pressure~\cite{berntson1997};
and walking to elevate HR with characteristic recovery kinetics while
compromising fine-scale HRV through motion artifact (H3).
For paced breathing, classical resonance theory predicts peak RR
amplitude near 6~breaths/min (H4)~\cite{lehrer2014}, and the
spectral-band migration critique predicts that LF/HF will inflate
when breathing slows below the 0.15-Hz boundary, without a
corresponding change in RMSSD (H5)~\cite{billman2013,eckberg1997}.
Entrainment of the RR spectral peak by paced breathing was verified
by linear regression.

All recordings were processed through the same preprocessing and
R-peak detection pipeline, and per-condition metric validity is
summarized in Table~\ref{tab:validity}.

\section{Materials and Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Study Design and Protocol}
\label{ssec:protocol}

One healthy adult male participant (age 22, no known cardiovascular
or respiratory conditions) was instrumented with a VitalSigns VS-ECG
single-lead chest-patch sensor sampling ECG at 500~Hz and triaxial
accelerometry at 25~Hz.
All 14~recordings were acquired in a single session on April~24,
2026, under quiet indoor conditions.

The protocol comprised four experiment families
(Table~\ref{tab:protocol}):

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Postural baseline (E1).}
  After a 5-min rest, three consecutive 5-min recordings were obtained
  in supine (E1A), sitting (E1B), and standing (E1C) postures.
  The participant maintained relaxed spontaneous breathing throughout.
  Because 30-s segments provide poor LF resolution
  ($\sim$2 resolvable bins~\cite{oppenheim1999}), each posture was
  extended to 5~min following Task Force
  guidelines~\cite{taskforce1996}, providing $\Delta f \approx
  0.005$~Hz and $\sim$22 LF-band bins.
  Window-duration effects on metric stability are quantified in
  Appendix~A (Fig.~\ref{fig:duration_effect}).

\item \textbf{Voluntary breath-hold (E2).}
  In a seated position, the participant performed two trials of
  maximum inspiratory breath-hold ($\sim$40~s each) followed by 40~s
  of recovery breathing, and two trials of maximum expiratory
  breath-hold with the same protocol.
  Hold duration was extended to $\sim$40~s (vs.\ the 20-s assignment
  specification) to increase cardiac cycles available for hold-phase
  metrics~\cite{taskforce1996}.
  Inter-trial rest was provided until baseline HR was restored.
  Within each respiratory mode, two consecutive trials were obtained.
  Trial~1 was performed with greater volitional effort (anticipatory
  strain), while Trial~2 was more relaxed, consistent with the known
  attenuation of volitional strain on repetition~\cite{berntson1997}.
  Both trials are reported in Table~\ref{tab:breathhold}; spectral
  quantification uses only Trial~2 because Trial~1 violated
  stationarity, evidenced by HR-slope sign reversal in the inspiratory
  condition ($-$0.19 vs.\ $+$0.18~bpm/s;
  Table~\ref{tab:breathhold}).

\item \textbf{Walking challenge (E3).}
  A 180-s continuous recording was segmented into 60~s seated
  baseline, 60~s walking at self-selected pace, and 60~s seated
  recovery.

\item \textbf{Paced breathing (E4).}
  \emph{Self-designed variation (Assignment Q4).}
  This self-designed variation tested \textbf{H4} (resonance) and
  \textbf{H5} (spectral-band migration under slow respiration).
  Five conditions were recorded in a seated position at fixed
  breathing rates of 12, 9, 6, 5, and 3~breaths/min, guided by a
  metronome.
  Each condition lasted approximately 4~min; the 3-breaths/min
  condition was extended to $\sim$5~min to ensure $\geq$14 complete
  breathing cycles.
  Inter-condition rest periods of $\geq$3~min were enforced to
  allow autonomic recovery and mitigate cumulative fatigue.
  The fixed descending order (12$\to$3~breaths/min) remains a
  potential source of order effects; progressive relaxation cannot
  be fully excluded without counterbalanced presentation.

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Signal Processing}
\label{ssec:sigproc}

All processing was performed in Python~3.10 using a unified pipeline
(\texttt{src/pipeline.py}).
Raw ECG was corrected for baseline drift with a two-stage median
filter (200~ms and 600~ms kernel widths), then filtered with a 60~Hz
second-order IIR notch ($Q = 30$) to suppress power-line interference
(Taiwan utility frequency),
followed by a 0.5--40~Hz fourth-order Butterworth bandpass
(zero-phase via \texttt{sosfiltfilt})~\cite{virtanen2020}.

R-peak detection followed two parallel paths to assess agreement
between detection approaches (Fig.~\ref{fig:pipeline_overview}):
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Independent validation pipeline}: a Pan--Tompkins-style
  detector implemented in SciPy~\cite{pan1985}, providing a
  transparent, interpretable cross-check.
\item \emph{Primary analysis pipeline}: NeuroKit2 \texttt{ecg\_peaks}
  with Kubios-style artifact correction~\cite{makowski2021}, serving
  as the primary source for all reported results because it includes
  established artifact-correction routines for transient segments.
\end{itemize}
Pipeline validation on a 5-min steady-state recording (E1A)
showed $\geq$99.5\% peak-count agreement between paths, with
mean RR difference $<$1.0~ms and RMSSD difference $<$2.7~ms.

\subsection{HRV Analysis}
\label{ssec:hrv}

HRV indices were computed following the Task Force
standards~\cite{taskforce1996}.
RR intervals outside the 300--2000~ms physiological range were
rejected.
For steady-state conditions (E1, E4), the full analysis window
($\geq$220~s) was used.
Window-length sensitivity on the E1A supine recording (Appendix~A,
Fig.~\ref{fig:duration_effect}) confirmed that CV fell below 10\%
for SDNN and below 5\% for HF beyond $\sim$180~s, supporting the
use of full multi-minute analysis windows.

\textbf{Time domain.}
The following time-domain indices were computed from the edited NN
interval series following Task Force
definitions~\cite{taskforce1996}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{SDNN}: the standard deviation of all NN intervals,
    reflecting total HRV encompassing both sympathetic and
    parasympathetic contributions.  Because SDNN is algebraically
    dependent on recording length, values are comparable only across
    segments of equal duration~\cite{taskforce1996}.
  \item \textbf{RMSSD}: the root mean square of successive NN
    interval differences, capturing short-term beat-to-beat
    variability mediated predominantly by vagal
    efferent activity~\cite{taskforce1996,shaffer2017}.  Unlike
    HF power, RMSSD is insensitive to spectral-band definitions
    and remains interpretable when breathing frequency migrates
    across the LF/HF boundary.
  \item \textbf{pNN50}: the proportion of successive NN interval
    pairs differing by more than 50~ms, a complementary
    parasympathetic index highly correlated with RMSSD but employing
    a threshold criterion rather than a continuous
    measure~\cite{taskforce1996}.
\end{itemize}
Mean heart rate (HR) in beats per minute was computed as
$60 / \overline{\text{NN}}$, where $\overline{\text{NN}}$ is the mean
normal-to-normal interval in seconds.

\textbf{Frequency domain.} The RR series was linearly detrended and
resampled at 4 Hz via cubic interpolation \cite{taskforce1996}. Power
spectral density (PSD) was estimated using Welch's method with a Hann
window and 50\% overlap. The window length \texttt{nperseg} was selected
according to recording duration: 256 points ($\Delta f \approx 0.0156$
Hz) for the 5-min postural baselines (E1) and breath-hold per-trial
spectra (E2), and 512 points ($\Delta f \approx 0.0078$ Hz) for the
$\geq$220 s paced-breathing recordings (E4) to improve frequency
resolution near the 0.15 Hz LF/HF boundary, which is critical for the
band-migration analysis (H5). In all cases, $\geq$8 non-overlapping
window equivalents were available, ensuring stable spectral averaging.
Band powers were integrated over the Task Force frequency ranges: VLF
(0.003--0.04 Hz), LF (0.04--0.15 Hz), and HF (0.15--0.40 Hz). Normalized
units were computed as $\text{LF}_{\text{n.u.}} = \text{LF}/(\text{TP}
- \text{VLF}) \times 100$.

\textbf{Transient conditions (E2, E3).}
Because individual phases (hold, walk, recovery) lasted only
40--60~s, standard frequency-domain HRV was not computed
per-phase~\cite{taskforce1996}.
Instead, we reported regime-based mean~HR, RMSSD, HR~range, and
HR~slope.
For E2, frequency-domain HRV indices (HF power, LF/HF) were
computed per trial using the same Welch method, and spectral
comparison was performed against the E1B seated anchor.

\textbf{Walking recovery kinetics.}
Post-walking HR recovery was fitted to a single-exponential decay
model:
\begin{equation}
  \text{HR}(t) = \text{HR}_{\infty}
  + (\text{HR}_{0} - \text{HR}_{\infty})\,
    e^{-t/\tau},
\label{eq:recovery}
\end{equation}
using nonlinear least-squares (\texttt{scipy.optimize.curve\_fit})
over the 60-s post-walking recovery window.

\textbf{Motion artifact assessment.}
Walking-phase motion artifact was evaluated using two
complementary metrics.
First, Pearson correlation between absolute successive RR
differences ($|\Delta\text{RR}|$) and accelerometer vector magnitude
was computed during the walking segment ($r = 0.26$).
Second, magnitude-squared coherence ($C_{xy}$) between the walking
ECG and accelerometer-derived motion magnitude was estimated to test
whether a stable, phase-locked step-frequency component coupled into
the cardiac signal.
Because coherence captures phase-locked linear coupling better than
amplitude correlation, both measures are interpreted jointly in
Section~\ref{ssec:walking}.

\textbf{Paced-breathing frequency tracking.}
The dominant RR spectral peak was identified for each paced-breathing
condition and compared against the imposed breathing frequency using
ordinary least-squares regression.
Slope, intercept, $R^{2}$, and 95\% confidence intervals were
reported; the null hypotheses slope~$=1$ and intercept~$=0$ were
tested.

\textbf{Respiratory-centered spectral analysis.}
To assess resonance independently of fixed LF/HF boundaries,
respiratory-centered RR spectral power was computed by integrating
the Welch PSD within $\pm$0.015~Hz of each imposed breathing
frequency; sensitivity analyses used $\pm$0.010~Hz and
$\pm$0.020~Hz windows.
RR oscillation amplitude was additionally quantified as the
distribution-based p95--p5 range, robust to cycle-level detection
instability without a direct respiratory signal.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Postural Loading Reproduced the Expected Orthostatic HRV Pattern}
\label{ssec:posture}

Orthostatic loading was associated with monotonic changes in HRV
metrics (Table~\ref{tab:posture},
Fig.~\ref{fig:posture}--\ref{fig:poincare}).
From supine to standing, mean HR increased from 54.0 to 79.7~bpm,
RMSSD decreased from 99.1 to 25.2~ms, HF power dropped from 3252.8
to 310.7~ms$^{2}$, and LF/HF rose from 0.28 to 1.44.
This pattern is compatible with orthostatic vagal
withdrawal~\cite{taskforce1996}.

The ECG power spectral density (Fig.~\ref{fig:ecg_psd}) further
reveals the heartbeat fundamental frequency ($f_{0}$) and its
harmonics ($2f_{0}$, $3f_{0}$), along with respiratory modulation
visible as a low-frequency spectral peak, across all three postures.

Poincar\'e return maps (Fig.~\ref{fig:poincare}) show the same
reduction in beat-to-beat variability: the
scatter cloud contracts progressively from supine to standing,
mirroring the RMSSD reduction in
Table~\ref{tab:posture}.

The sitting baseline (E1B) was subsequently used as the
posture-matched anchor for the seated breath-hold and walking
experiments, reducing confounding by posture in between-experiment spectral
comparisons.

% ------ Breath-Hold ------
\subsection{Breath-Hold Produced Asymmetric Inspiratory--Expiratory HR Responses}
\label{ssec:breathhold}

In this participant, inspiratory breath-hold produced larger HR
responses than expiratory hold across both trials (Table~\ref{tab:breathhold},
Fig.~\ref{fig:breathhold}).
Across two inspiratory trials, mean hold-phase HR rose to 66.9 and
73.5~bpm ($\Delta$HR = +4.0 and +14.3~bpm relative to pre-hold
baseline), whereas expiratory hold-phase HR remained near baseline
(57.8 and 60.8~bpm; $\Delta$HR = $-$0.9 and +1.9~bpm).
The large inter-trial variability in the inspiratory condition
(CV of pre-hold HR: 4.3\% vs.\ 0.2\% for expiratory) suggests that
conscious effort and volitional strategy, not just
respiratory cessation, shaped the breath-hold response in this
participant.

Spectral comparison against the E1B seated anchor showed a similar
asymmetry (Fig.~\ref{fig:hf_collapse}).
To reduce the influence of volitional effort, only the relaxed trial
(Trial~2) was used for spectral
quantification; the effort confound is addressed separately in
Fig.~\ref{fig:breathhold}.
Inspiratory hold HF power decreased markedly to 39.1~ms$^{2}$ (a 28.7-fold
reduction from the anchor value of 1123~ms$^{2}$; $-$96.5\%),
whereas expiratory hold HF fell to 230.9~ms$^{2}$
(4.9-fold; $-$79.4\%).
Both conditions recovered to near-anchor levels within the 50~s
post-hold window (inspiratory: 1207~ms$^{2}$, $\times$1.07;
expiratory: 1213~ms$^{2}$, $\times$1.08), suggesting a transient
perturbation with recovery toward the seated anchor.

% ------ Walking ------
\subsection{Walking Increased Heart Rate but Limited Fine-Scale HRV Interpretation}
\label{ssec:walking}

The walking challenge produced an acute cardiovascular response
consistent with mild exercise (Table~\ref{tab:walking},
Figs.~\ref{fig:walk_motion},~\ref{fig:recovery_tau}).
Mean HR rose from 60.8 to 83.0~bpm and returned to 70.3~bpm
during recovery ($\tau = 6.8$~s, Eq.~\ref{eq:recovery}),
indicating rapid parasympathetic
reactivation~\cite{shaffer2017}.

Walking-phase RMSSD (41.1~ms) should be interpreted cautiously.
Motion artifacts---electrode-skin impedance changes, trunk EMG,
cable sway---can introduce fiducial-point jitter and inflate
beat-to-beat variability. The accelerometer-derived step frequency
(1.46~Hz) fell within 6\% of the cardiac fundamental
($f_0 = 1.38$~Hz), creating spectral overlap that linear
filtering cannot resolve (Fig.~\ref{fig:walk_ecg_psd}).
Magnitude-squared coherence at the step peak was low
($C_{xy} = 0.04$; 1--3~Hz band mean $C_{xy} = 0.06$), and
the correlation between $|\Delta\text{RR}|$ and accelerometer
magnitude was weak ($r = 0.26$). R-peak detection remained
nominally successful (Appendix~D,
Fig.~\ref{fig:walking_ecg_processing}), but low coherence does
not exclude broadband drift. We therefore treat walking-phase
fine-scale HRV as motion-limited: RMSSD is reported but not
used as a cross-condition comparator.

% ------ Paced Breathing ------
\subsection{Paced Breathing Shifted RR Spectral Power Across Frequency Bands}
\label{ssec:paced}

The paced-breathing experiment yielded three main observations.

\subsubsection{Frequency Entrainment Was Quantitatively Verified}

The measured RR spectral peak closely tracked the imposed breathing
frequency (Fig.~\ref{fig:psd_overlay}; Appendix~A,
Fig.~\ref{fig:freq_entrainment}):
slope~$= 1.022$ (95\%~CI: 0.947--1.097),
intercept~$= -0.002$~Hz (95\%~CI: $-$0.012 to 0.008),
$R^{2} = 0.998$, residual SD~$= 0.003$~Hz.
Neither the slope-$=$1 nor intercept-$=$0 null was rejected,
confirming respiratory entrainment.

\subsubsection{Slow Breathing Increased RR Oscillation Amplitude}

Mean HR varied narrowly across conditions (57.0--60.1~bpm), whereas
SDNN rose from 48.7 to 131.4~ms, total power from 1754 to
10{,}493~ms$^{2}$, and peak-to-trough RR amplitude from 157 to
406~ms (Table~\ref{tab:paced}, Fig.~\ref{fig:e4a_tachograms}).
Slow breathing thus primarily expanded oscillatory amplitude rather
than shifting mean heart rate.

\subsubsection{Respiratory-Centered Spectral Power Did Not Peak at
6~Breaths/Min (\textbf{H4} Not Supported)}

Classical resonance biofeedback theory predicts a peak in RR
oscillation amplitude near 6~breaths/min
($\sim$0.1~Hz)~\cite{lehrer2014}.
To test this without relying on fixed LF/HF bands or cycle-level
peak-to-trough detection (which can be unstable during very slow
breathing without a direct respiratory belt signal), we computed
respiratory-centered RR spectral power by integrating the Welch PSD
within $\pm$0.015~Hz of each imposed breathing frequency.
Both respiratory-centered power and distribution-based RR amplitude
(p95--p5) increased monotonically from 12~breaths/min to
3~breaths/min (1003 to 13{,}460~ms$^{2}$ and 157 to 406~ms,
respectively; Fig.~\ref{fig:e4a_resp_resonance}), without forming
an inverted-U shape centered at 6~breaths/min.
Sensitivity analysis using $\pm$0.010~Hz and $\pm$0.020~Hz
integration windows showed that the same monotonic pattern was
observed across all three bandwidths
(Appendix E, Fig.~\ref{fig:resonance_sensitivity}).
RR oscillation amplitude thus increased monotonically as breathing
slowed, without showing the inverted-U profile centered at
6~breaths/min predicted by classical resonance theory. The
fixed descending presentation order qualifies this finding
(Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).

\subsubsection{LF/HF Inflation Dissociated from RMSSD}

LF/HF rose from 0.22 (12/min) to 11.54 (6/min) and
plateaued near 17.5 at the slowest rates, yet RMSSD remained in a
narrow range of 48--66~ms throughout (Fig.~\ref{fig:lfhf_rmssd}).
Because the respiratory peak crossed the 0.15-Hz boundary between
these conditions, the LF/HF increase is better explained by
spectral-band migration than by a sympathovagal
shift~\cite{billman2013}.
RMSSD, computed entirely in the time domain, is unaffected by
frequency-band assignment and provided a more stable vagal index
across these conditions.

% ------ Cross-Experiment ------
\subsection{HRV Metrics Behaved Differently Across Tasks}
\label{ssec:integration}

Viewed together (Fig.~\ref{fig:integration}), the four experiments
reveal two distinct response patterns.
\emph{Posture} primarily shifted the autonomic operating point along
a trajectory of increasing HR and decreasing RMSSD (supine
$\to$ standing), with total power declining monotonically.
\emph{Paced breathing}, by contrast, maintained nearly stable
mean~HR while expanding total power by a factor of 6.0
(1754$\to$10{,}493~ms$^{2}$) and SDNN by a factor of 2.7.

These represent different types of physiological modulation
and cannot be ranked on a single LF/HF axis.
The breath-hold and walking paradigms are better described by
their transient HR dynamics: inspiratory hold showed the
larger HR rise, and walking recovery followed an exponential
time course ($\tau = 6.8$~s).

Cross-experiment summaries are therefore more informative when
reported as mean~HR, RMSSD, and total power with explicit validity
annotations (e.g., ``motion-limited,'' ``band-shifted'') rather
than reduced to a single frequency-domain ratio.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Context Determines Interpretation}

HRV indices did not retain the same meaning across tasks.
LF/HF separated supine from standing (0.28 vs.\ 1.44) but reached
17.47 under slow paced breathing, where it reflected relocation of the
respiratory peak into the LF band rather than sympathovagal change.
A single participant thus produced a 60-fold LF/HF range through
postural and respiratory manipulations
alone~\cite{billman2013,eckberg1997}.

\subsection{Paced Breathing: Amplitude, Not Rate}

The most prominent effect of slow breathing was amplitude expansion
of respiratory sinus arrhythmia rather than reduction of mean heart
rate: mean HR varied by only 3.1~bpm across the five paced conditions
(57.0--60.1~bpm), whereas peak-to-trough RR amplitude increased by
159\% (157--406~ms).
Slow breathing thus amplified cardiorespiratory coupling---baroreflex
modulation, respiratory gating of vagal
efferents~\cite{hirsch1981}, and intrathoracic pressure
oscillations---without substantially shifting mean
HR~\cite{berntson1997,penttila2001}.

The absence of a 6-breaths/min resonance maximum requires
qualification. Classical theory~\cite{lehrer2014} predicts peak RR
amplitude near 0.1~Hz. Using respiratory-centered spectral power,
both spectral power and RR amplitude (p95--p5) increased
monotonically to 3~breaths/min (0.05~Hz), unchanged across three
integration bandwidths ($\pm$0.010, $\pm$0.015, $\pm$0.020~Hz).
However, the fixed descending presentation order
(12$\to$3~breaths/min) means the monotonic increase may partly
reflect progressive relaxation or deeper tidal volumes.
A counterbalanced multi-participant design with respiratory
monitoring would be needed to distinguish order effects from a true
resonance-frequency shift.

\subsection{Reporting Recommendations}

Three practices follow from the present analysis:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Report LF/HF only when breathing rate is confirmed to lie
  within the HF band ($>$0.15~Hz, i.e., $>$9~breaths/min).
  Otherwise, use RMSSD or total power as the primary summary.
\item Annotate each condition with a validity note indicating
  whether the standard interpretation applies (e.g.,
  ``standard,'' ``band-shifted,'' ``motion-limited'').
\item Use posture-matched anchors when comparing transient tasks
  against baselines, to avoid confounding postural and
  task-specific autonomic effects.
\end{enumerate}

in each condition.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Several limitations should be considered. This is a single-participant
study, so the findings illustrate metric-behavior principles under
controlled within-subject manipulation but cannot be generalized
to a broader population. The paced-breathing conditions were
presented in fixed descending order (12$\to$3~breaths/min);
despite $\geq$3-min recovery periods between conditions, progressive
relaxation or training effects cannot be ruled out without a
counterbalanced design.

Three further constraints affect interpretation. No direct
respiratory signal was recorded, so entrainment was inferred from
the RR spectral peak rather than measured by a respiratory belt or
impedance pneumography. Walking-phase fine-scale HRV is
motion-limited: low coherence between ECG and accelerometer
($C_{xy} = 0.04$) ruled out strong phase-locked coupling but does
not exclude broadband artifact, so walking RMSSD was not used for
cross-condition comparison. Finally, each condition was recorded
once (twice for breath-hold), precluding within-condition
reliability assessment; absolute values are session-specific, and
replication would strengthen the methodological conclusions about
relative metric behavior across tasks~\cite{shaffer2017}.


% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

Across four physiological tasks, the same HRV metrics changed for
different reasons. Postural loading primarily shifted the autonomic
operating point, with higher heart rate and lower RMSSD from supine to
standing. Walking produced a clear heart-rate response and rapid
recovery, but cadence--cardiac spectral proximity made fine-scale HRV
during ambulation difficult to interpret. In paced breathing, the
dominant RR spectral peak tracked the imposed breathing rate, but its
movement across conventional LF/HF boundaries changed the meaning of
spectral indices.

The clearest interpretive failure occurred during slow paced breathing:
LF/HF increased from 0.22 to 17.47 while RMSSD remained relatively
stable, indicating spectral-band migration rather than a true
sympathovagal shift. RR oscillation amplitude also increased
monotonically as breathing slowed, rather than peaking at
6~breaths/min, although this finding should be interpreted in light of
the fixed descending protocol. Overall, these results show that HRV
metrics should be selected according to the recording condition,
rather than interpreted uniformly across tasks. For slow breathing and
motion-contaminated recordings, explicit validity notes and
posture-matched comparisons are more informative than relying on LF/HF
alone.

\section{Paced Breathing Resonance: Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{app:resonance}

The respiratory-centered RR spectral power metric introduced in
Section~\ref{ssec:paced} depends on the choice of integration
bandwidth around each imposed breathing frequency. To verify that our
central conclusion (absence of a 6-breaths/min resonance
maximum) is not an artifact of bandwidth selection, we repeated the
analysis with three integration windows: $\pm$0.010, $\pm$0.015
(primary), and $\pm$0.020~Hz.

Figure~\ref{fig:resonance_sensitivity} shows that the wider
$\pm$0.015 and $\pm$0.020~Hz bandwidths yield a monotonic decrease in
respiratory-centered power from 3 to 12~breaths/min, with no isolated
peak at 6~breaths/min. The narrower $\pm$0.010~Hz window shows lower
absolute power, with a noticeable drop at 5~breaths/min, because some
respiratory-band energy falls outside the integration limits where
peaks shift or broaden, but the overall conclusion (and notably, the
absence of an inverted-U) is preserved.

The monotonic pattern was thus robust to bandwidth choice, though
fixed-order effects remain a possible confound.

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{nb05_fig05_e4a_resp_centered_power.pdf}
\caption{Sensitivity analysis of respiratory-centered RR spectral power
using $\pm$0.010, $\pm$0.015, and $\pm$0.020~Hz integration windows.
The $\pm$0.015 and $\pm$0.020~Hz bandwidths showed monotonically
increasing power from 12 to 3~breaths/min with no isolated maximum at
6~breaths/min.}
\label{fig:resonance_sensitivity}
\end{figure}
